SIP Governance Framework

To Allocate Decision Rights, We Use the RAPID® Framework

Recommend
  • Agree on the criteria, required burden of proof, and decision process with the ‘D’ upfront
  • Consult all ‘I’s early enough in the process
  • Give all input due consideration
  • Involve any ‘A’s effectively
  • Work in a collaborative manner
Agree
  • Engage in the process in a constructive fashion (e.g., work with the ‘R’ to avoid any last minute conflict)
  • Work collaboratively to resolve issues, develop alternatives if can’t approve a recommendation
  • Use the ‘A’ on appropriate basis
Perform
  • Move into prompt execution, even if the decision isn’t in line with your views
  • Don’t reopen the decision unless there is a major change or unforeseen circumstances
  • Put in feedback loops and respond appropriately
Input
  • Provide input and considered opinions in a thoughtful, timely manner
  • Don’t insist on having a greater role – ‘I’s are not ‘A’s
  • If the decision isn’t in line with your views, support it (‘Disagree and commit’)
Decide
  • Establish criteria and expectations for the process upfront
  • Consider recommendation and input carefully
  • Be decisive
  • Ensure people respect decision roles
  • Communicate decision and rationale and ensure resources set up for execution

Program Management: Scenarios to Govern

Plan Participation Norms

Moves onto Plan:

  • New Hires (External and Internal)

Moves off of Plan:

  • Lateral Transfers
  • Promotions
  • Transition to Non-Sales Job
  • Involuntary Termination and Resignations

Time Away from Plan:

  • Leaves of Absence
    • Long-Term
    • Short-Term
  • Vacation
  • Open Territories / Accounts

Considerations

Inputs to Decision

  • Frequency of Performance and Pay Periods
  • Level of Manager Discretion in Awards
  • Local / Legal Requirements

Impacts from Decision

  • Plan Crediting or Goal Setting Impacts for remaining team

Plan Crediting Considerations

Exceptional Situations:

  • Management rights – Managed through Governance decisions
  • Quota changes - Managed through Governance decisions
  • Employee Moves – Managed through Plan Participation norms

Windfall deals/mega deals:

  • To protect against unexpected excessive payouts from mega orders,  consider the following options: 
    • Provide a “major order” incentive schedule that limits quota crediting but provides for a modest incentive payment for volume beyond a pre-determined level
    • Apply a cap to these earnings
    • Apply a significant decelerators to plan funding past the determined point of excellent performance

Plan elements tied to strategic outcomes accelerated whereas individual employee compensation impacts typically transitioned over time.

Pay Mix Transition Options – To More At Risk

The following table outlines transition options to migrate to a MORE aggressive pay mix. For example, migrating a Rep with an 80/20 pay mix to 70/30.
Option Pros Cons

Cold Cut – Reduce salary and increase target incentive
  • ✓
    Simple to administer
  • ✓
    Quickly aligns individual to new pay mix
  • ×
    Negative employee perception due to base salary cut

Buy Out – Provide individual a one time bonus to buy out of existing salary structure/role
  • ✓
    Eases transition for rep to reduced base pay
  • ✓
    Quickly aligns individual to new pay mix
  • ×
    Expensive, especially if multiple individuals are being transitioned

Migrate over Time – Migrate individuals to new pay mix over time. E.g., convert 5% of salary to target incentive each year for 3 years
  • ✓
    Somewhat simple to administer; need to keep track of variances over time
  • ✓
    No negative employee perceptions
  • ✓
    Quicker transition than “merit increase” option
  • ×
    Pay Mix does not reflect the level of required individual persuasion for a certain period
  • ×
    Varied pay mixes within a job for a certain period

Migrate with Merit Increase – Add all future merit increase to the target incentive until individual at the new mix
  • ✓
    Somewhat simple to administer; need to keep track of variances over time
  • ✓
    No negative employee perceptions
  • ×
    Pay Mix does not reflect the level of required individual persuasion for a certain period
  • ×
    Varied pay mixes within a job for a certain period
  • ×
    May take a long time

Legacy – Honor the individual at the old pay mix
  • ✓
    Somewhat simple to administer; need to keep track of variances
  • ✓
    No negative employee perceptions
  • ×
    Pay Mix does not reflect the level of required individual persuasion
  • ×
    Varied pay mixes within a job

Moving to new pay mix; Less than +/- 5% Change

(e.g., 84/26 to 80/20)
  • Increase TTC to minimum range
  • Apply increase to base¹ or target incentive to migrate to correct pay mix
  • May use merit increase at focal to correct pay mix (if needed)
  • No change to TTC
  • Use merit increase at focal to correct pay mix
  • Decrease TTC to top of range (Consider a buy-out)
  • Apply decrease to base or target incentive to migrate to correct pay mix
  • Use merit increase at focal to correct pay mix (if needed)
  • Allow ample time to communicate any pay change and/or performance period change prior to implementation
  • Work with local ER/IR and Legal resources to determine acceptable actions for country
  • Migrate up to 15% of pay every 6-12 months (depending on performance period) until achieve desired pay levels
  • Work with Finance to ensure availability of budget
  • Use focal budget to correct pay mix whenever possible

Risks & Mitigations

The following table outlines transition options to migrate to a LESS aggressive pay mix. For example, migrating a Rep with a 50/50 pay mix to 70/30.
Risk Mitigation

Lack of Buy-in

  • Sales leaders do not take ownership of the plans
  • Sellers do not believe in plans, therefore, lose line-of-sight into strategy
  • Ensure sales leaders own the plans and clearly communicate alignment, expectations and how to achieve success

Plan Complexity / Poor Communication

  • Sellers are not able to easily understand how they are paid out or how to be “successful”
  • Develop multi-phase communication plan that incorporates executives, sales leaders and front-line managers
  • Ensure management understands payout calculations
  • Provide clear plan documents and comp estimators
  • Prioritize most important measures, ensuring no more than three

Incorrect Goal-setting / Goal-allocation

  • Individual targeted expectations do not match opportunities within a given territory or geography
  • Earnings and sales costs fluctuate
  • Market conditions change
  • Establish clear goal-allocation methodologies
  • Communicate definition of goal, excellence
  • Where appropriate, shorten measure period to reflect market volatility
  • Incorporate thresholds and decelerators

Insufficient Sales Comp Admin. and Governance

  • Lack of a complete governance model
  • No continuity of plan implementation and oversight
  • Develop complete set of business rules, governance and administration guidelines and goal allocation procedures

Over Reliance on Comp to get Sales Results

  • Other, more effective management tools ignored
  • Develop a core set of sales effectiveness tools (e.g. opportunity modeling, account / territory planning, job role clarification, coach models, competency assessments and holistic education)

New Plan Components Don’t Drive Right Behaviors
  • Sales managers monitor performance and behaviors monthly and report to leadership
  • Conduct quarterly formal reviews
