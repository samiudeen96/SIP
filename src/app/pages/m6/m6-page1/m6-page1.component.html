Target Setting Guiding Principles

BEST PRACTICES
  • Sales targets are based on top-down business goals and bottom-up customer revenue opportunity
  • A uniform method of allocating sales targets is employed across the organization
  • The sales force understands how their targets were developed
  • That sales force receives their targets prior to the start of the performance period
  • The distribution of performance against target is predictable with a consistent percentage of people over and underachieving each year.
CHALLENGES
  • Changing sales organization designs, territory assignments and sales crediting rules.
  • Reliance on availability and accuracy of data
  • Reflecting capability of sales force and experience level of sales person
  • Capturing unique aspects of local markets and accounts/territories
  • Reliance on business planning process and sales/marketing plans (timing)

Target Setting: Ideal Attainment Distribution

Best practice distribution of goal attainment results in 60-70% of sellers achieving target and 30-40% of sellers below target, with 80% of sellers between threshold and excellence.

DO’s and DON’Ts of Setting Targets

DO’s
  • Keep the target setting and allocation methodology as simple as possible
  • Establish and allocate sales targets incorporating input from the “bottoms-up” and from “top-down”
  • Fully allocate the business plan
  • Establish a clear statement of target setting policies
  • Explain the sales target setting and allocation process to sellers
  • Establish an audit process to track effectiveness of the current target setting and allocation process
  • Reassess the target setting and allocation methodologies prior to the start of each year
DON’Ts
  • Don’t tie the volatility of incentive payouts to small fluctuations in quota performance (i.e. it would typically be inappropriate for a 1% increase in quota performance to result in a 20% increase (or greater) in incentive payout.)
  • Don’t assign sales targets based on sales history alone unless products and markets have maintained a consistent, slow-growth trend
  • Don’t assume the target setting process will manage itself. Monitor often and adjust as needed.

Target Setting Approaches

There are three basic approaches that sales management can use to effectively allocate sales targets among the sales force.
  1. No distinction between sales professionals: Total organization or sales team quota divided by the number of sales professionals equals each individual quota
  2. Same rate increase: Assign the same percentage growth rate to the base number for each sales manager, each sales professional, and each overlay position
  3. Individual targets: Assign targets based on individual sales territory, productivity and opportunity. It recognizes that sales opportunities vary by individual. Requires more in-depth knowledge about each individual’s sales opportunities and selling capabilities. Approached for setting individual targets include the following:
    • Opportunity Planning
    • Major Account Planning
    • Modified Fair Share
    • Goaling Grid
    • Statistical Modeling

Modified Fair Share – Examples of Modifiers

Modifying Factors: Modifying Factors are factors that we expect to have an influence (up or down) on an individual’s ability to achieve a sales goal. Below is a list of common factors used to modify targets.
Potential Factors Assumption
Sales Potential (Total Available Market) It is easier to attain one’s target in a territory with a high sales potential
Market Share It is more difficult to attain sales in territories with high market share of company’s products
Share of Wallet (Customer Share) It is more difficult to attain sales in accounts with high customer share of company’s products
Expected Value of Current Deals in the Funnel It is easier to attain one’s target if the expected value of the current deals in the funnel are high
Historical Sales Performance Territories with high sales performance in the past should perform well in the future
Competitive Intensity It is more difficult to attain sales in territories with high levels of competition
Economic Growth Customers, industries, geographies with higher economic growth tend to buy more
Years with Firm Individuals with more tenure at company have an easier time selling company’s products
Sales Experience Individuals with more sales experience should have an easier time selling
Experience in Region/Territory It is easier to attain sales in territories where the individual has account/buyer knowledge and experience
Pros
  • ✓
    Easy to calculate and understood by Reps (assuming modifiers have been explained)
  • ✓
    Uses readily accessible data
  • ✓
    Considers potential factors in addition to historical data
  • ✓
    Includes management in the process
Cons
  • ×
    Requires robust management training
  • ×
    Requires management knowledge of potential factors and how to apply factors to historical results
  • ×
    Still reliant on historical data

5. Statistical Modeling

Statistical Modeling uses external (and internal) data and regression analytics to predict appropriate quotas.
Step 1
Collect all territory quantitative data, including global variables and customer/product variables.
Step 2
Develop a mathematical formula (algorithm‘) based on correlation of variables and regression analysis.
Step 3
Conduct algorithm solution to establish sales quotas by account. Adjust for un-measurable factors. Adjust for personnel changes.
Pros
  • ✓
    Can be very precise and accurate
  • ✓
    Consistent and objective process
  • ✓
    Minimal sales management involvement required
Cons
  • ×
    Difficult to communicate how quotas are set
  • ×
    Reliant on accurate data / data analytics with high correlation to results
